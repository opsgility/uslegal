# Prompt Library — US Legal Sales Team
---

This is your take-home reference. Keep it open alongside your tools. Every prompt below follows the **Goal → Context → Sources → Expectations** framework. Customize the bracketed fields for your specific situation.

> **How to use this library:** Copy a prompt, replace the `[bracketed]` text with your details, paste it into Copilot, and iterate. The best results come from prompts where you provide specific context — firm name, size, services, history. Generic prompts give generic results.

## Outreach & Prospecting (Outlook)

### Cold Prospecting

**First-touch email to a new firm:**
> I'm a Sales Representative at US Legal. Draft a cold prospecting email to [contact title] at [Firm Name], a [size]-attorney [practice focus] firm in [location]. They [specific detail — recent news, expansion, new hire, etc.]. Position US Legal as a partner that helps firms like theirs [specific value prop relevant to their situation]. Tone: knowledgeable peer, not vendor. Under 150 words. End with a soft ask for a 15-minute call.

**Research-informed outreach:**
> I'm reaching out to [Firm Name], a [size]-attorney firm that specializes in [practice area]. Based on my research, they [specific insight — expanding into new markets, recently added partners, known for high-volume litigation, etc.]. Draft an email that connects this insight to how US Legal's [specific service] could support their growth. Make the connection feel natural, not forced. Keep it brief.

**Follow-up after no response (2nd touch):**
> I emailed [contact name] at [Firm Name] two weeks ago about US Legal's [service]. They haven't responded. Draft a brief follow-up that doesn't feel pushy. Add one new piece of value — either an insight about their market, a relevant stat about firms like theirs, or a mention of what we've done for a similar firm. Under 100 words.

**Post-meeting follow-up:**
> I just had a [first call / meeting] with [contact name] at [Firm Name]. Key discussion points: [list 2-3]. They expressed interest in [service] but had concerns about [concern]. Draft a follow-up email that: (1) thanks them for their time, (2) recaps the key points, (3) directly addresses their concern, and (4) proposes a clear next step — [specific next step]. Tone: professional, confident, concise.

## Account Nurturing & Expansion (Outlook)

**Check-in that isn't "just checking in":**
> Draft a check-in email to [contact name] at [Firm Name], a current client that uses our [current services]. I haven't connected with them in [timeframe]. Don't use the phrase "just checking in." Instead, lead with [a relevant insight, a trend in their usage, an industry development, or a new feature we've launched]. Suggest a brief call to discuss. Under 120 words.

**Warm intro to a new contact at an existing firm:**
> Draft an introduction email to [New Contact Name], the new [title] at [Firm Name], a firm where US Legal has been a client for [timeframe]. We currently provide [services]. I want to introduce myself and our partnership without being salesy. Reference our existing relationship, congratulate them on their new role, and offer to share how the firm currently uses our services. Tone: warm, collegial, brief.

**Cross-sell / expand services:**
> Draft an email to [contact name] at [Firm Name] introducing our [new service]. They currently use [current services]. Position the new service as a natural extension — not an upsell. Include a specific benefit: [e.g., "firms that bundle retrieval and compliance filing see a 20% reduction in turnaround time"]. Suggest a brief demo or overview call. Tone: helpful advisor.

**Re-engaging a quiet account:**
> Draft an email to [contact name] at [Firm Name]. They've been a client but engagement has dropped over the past [timeframe]. I want to re-engage without being awkward about the gap. Lead with value — [new feature, improvement, industry insight, or a specific idea]. Make it easy to reconnect — offer a quick call, a usage review, or a "here's what's new" summary. Tone: genuine, not desperate.

**Partnership thank-you note:**
> Draft a genuine "thank you for your continued partnership" email to [contact name] at [Firm Name]. We've worked together for [timeframe] and they use [services]. Reference something specific we've accomplished together — don't make it generic. Close with a forward-looking statement about the next year. Tone: authentic and personal.

## Meeting Prep (Teams)

**Prospect first meeting prep:**
> I have a first meeting with [contact title] at [Firm Name], a [size]-attorney [practice area] firm in [location]. Create a meeting prep sheet with: (1) 5 discovery questions specific to their firm type, (2) 3 talking points positioning US Legal for their needs, (3) 2 potential objections and responses, and (4) 2-3 "if they say X, respond with Y" scenarios for common pushback.

**Existing client QBR prep:**
> I have a quarterly business review with [Firm Name]. [Attach account history doc.] Create a QBR prep sheet with: (1) Relationship summary and key highlights, (2) 3-4 discussion points based on recent usage trends, (3) One expansion opportunity to propose, (4) A 2-3 sentence opening statement that reinforces partnership value, and (5) One proactive "challenge topic" to address before they raise it.

**Competitive displacement meeting prep:**
> I'm meeting with [Firm Name], who currently uses [Competitor Name]. They're considering alternatives because [reason — slow response times, pricing, service gaps]. Prepare a meeting sheet with: (1) Questions that surface dissatisfaction without badmouthing the competitor, (2) US Legal differentiators relevant to their frustration, (3) Talking points about our transition support, and (4) Responses to "we don't have time to switch."

## Meeting Summaries (Teams)

**Prospect meeting summary for HubSpot:**
> Analyze this meeting transcript. Extract: (1) Key pain points mentioned, (2) Services they're interested in, (3) Objections or concerns raised, (4) Next steps with owners and dates, (5) A 2-3 sentence deal summary for HubSpot. Format as a structured CRM note with short labels and bullet points.

**Client review summary for HubSpot:**
> Analyze this meeting transcript from a client review. Extract: (1) Client satisfaction signals (positive and negative), (2) Expansion opportunities discussed, (3) Action items with owners, (4) Risk signals (competitor mentions, budget concerns, dissatisfaction), (5) A brief CRM-ready summary. Format as a structured note for HubSpot.

## Competitive Intelligence & Analysis (Excel)

**Pricing analysis:**
> Summarize the key patterns in this competitive pricing data. Where does US Legal have a pricing advantage? Where are we vulnerable? Create a comparison table for the top 3 competitors across each service category.

**Account expansion potential:**
> Look at this account list and identify where I have the best expansion potential. Consider: firms using only 1-2 services, large firms with low contract values, and accounts with high engagement scores. Create an "Expansion Priority" ranking (High/Medium/Low) and a "Recommended Next Service" column.

**Prospect prioritization:**
> Help me prioritize which prospects to focus on this quarter. I can realistically pursue 8-10 firms. Rank the top 10 by: estimated deal value, competitive situation (unhappy with current provider = warmer), firm size, and region (where we have strong references). Explain your reasoning.

**Competitive response talking points:**
> Based on this pricing data, give me 3 talking points for when a prospect says "your competitor is cheaper." Focus on total cost of ownership, service bundling advantages, and quality/turnaround differences.

## Competitive Positioning & Sales Tools (Word)

**Battle card:**
> Create a competitive battle card comparing US Legal against [Competitor Name]. Include: (1) Quick comparison table across key dimensions, (2) Where we win (3-4 bullets), (3) Where they compete (honest assessment), (4) Objection handlers for: "happy with current provider," "your competitor is cheaper," "services are commoditized," "no time to transition," (5) Killer questions that expose competitor weaknesses. Keep it to 2 pages. Tone: honest, actionable — not marketing fluff.

**Expansion justification summary:**
> Create a one-page expansion justification for [Firm Name]. They currently use [services] and I want to pitch [new service]. Include: (1) Current partnership summary, (2) Recommended addition and why it's relevant, (3) Business case with specific benefits and benchmarks, (4) Low-commitment next step. Tone: trusted advisor.

**Value articulation (when rates are similar):**
> We're competing for [Firm Name]'s business and our rates are similar to [Competitor Name]. Give me 3 ways to articulate US Legal's value beyond price: (1) Service quality and relationship model, (2) Operational reliability and turnaround commitments, (3) Flexibility and customization for firms of their size.
